Frequently Asked Questions

Really, we get asked these often.

We get asked these questions a lot, so we made this small section to help you identify what you need faster.

How will I feel when am pregnant?

Everyone is different but hormones cause a lot of changes in your body and the way you feel. Some of us may have tender breasts, get tired easily or feel sick (any time of the day). Don’t worry if you feel up and down, it’s normal. An increase in the need to urinate and morning sickness will be more noticeable. You may also suffer from heartburn. In the second trimester, your breasts and the belly start to grow. You may feel the baby move and become more active.

When do pregnancy cravings start?

There isn’t a specific time when pregnancy food cravings start. It is different from every woman and you may not necessarily have any cravings. However, if you do have cravings, it could be in the first weeks of pregnancy, usually the first five weeks.

What if I have bleeding during pregnancy?

If you experience any bleeding, tell your midwife straight away and get her advice.

Can I exercise when pregnant?

Yes, you can! Staying active will help you control weight gain, improve fitness, reduce high blood pressure, improve sleep and improve your mood.

How will I know my due date?

You will need to know the date of your last menstrual period. 280 days or 40 weeks from the first day of your last period will be your due date. The due date is just an estimate. In reality, less than 5% of babies are born on the due date the mother is given. Many factors affect the due date of a child. Considering menstrual periods are not always like clockwork, just one day can throw off the due date.

When will I start to feel the baby kicking?

Most mums feel the first fidgets known as ‘quickening’ between weeks 14 and 26 of their pregnancy. However, it does vary and you’re advised to talk to the midwife or doctor about it.

What is the best way to reduce the baby’s risk of birth defects?
  • Folic acid is a vital nutrient necessary for the health and wellbeing of the newborn. It is essential for proper development.
  • The best way to reduce risk to your baby is to attend all your routinely scheduled prenatal appointments. The obstetrician is given a chance to closely monitor your pregnancy and the baby’s health.
  • Follow the doctor’s orders and only take supplements and medications they recommend. Certain medications are associated with birth defects and the bay being still born
